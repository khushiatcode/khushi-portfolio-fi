Why people

My parents speak Gujarati (a language from Gujarat, India). I grew up in Nagpur (a city in Maharashtra, India), so I learned Marathi (a language from Maharashtra, India) and Hindi (a language from India). School was in English. By the time I was ten, I had learned something most people spend careers chasing: every person carries an entire world of context inside them — and if you listen carefully enough, you can understand it.

That instinct has never left me. Now pursuing my Master's at Santa Clara University, I work daily alongside colleagues the US, China, India and across the world - and I find myself doing the same thing I've always done. Watching, translating, connecting dots, and quietly designing better experiences for the people around me.

"I don't just observe people. I build tools to serve them, design programs to delight them, and generate insights that help organizations understand them better."

At the Office of the Provost at SCU, I've automated workflows that used to take hours and sometimes days, rebuilt web experiences for the Office of the Provost at SCU, and designed engagement strategies based on who walks through the door and what they actually need. 1 🥿

The Shoe Research

I noticed something was off with mainstream shoe marketing. So I spent months studying biomechanics, physics, psychology, and consumer behavior — bought five different "best" pairs, tested them empirically, documented my findings, and ultimately convinced 14 people to rethink their footwear. All organically. Zero authority. Just rigorous, human-centered research and the ability to communicate findings to different audiences. Would love to discuss more and maybe I can convince you to rethink your footwear too!

What this really is
People Analytics → Behavior Change
2 🎨

Marketing a Friend's Art

A friend needed to sell her paintings. I researched her art style, identified her ideal buyer persona, studied what resonates with different collector types, and mediated between her and buyers — translating her artistic language into something buyers felt emotionally connected to. Sales happened. Relationships were built. No ads, no budget.

What this really is
Audience Research → Experience Design
3 🐾

Reading a Room (and a Retail Floor)

Working retail at SCU's BroncoStore, I noticed that Grandparents Week brought an older, more affluent audience than usual. Before anyone asked, I suggested we stock premium Peter Millar golf shirts on mannequin displays — the right product, the right placement, the right moment. That's the same instinct I bring to any space I'm responsible for: read who's in the room, and make it work for them.

What this really is
Spatial Intuition → Office Experience Design

How I turn
observation into
insight.

Whether I'm researching shoes, designing a web page, or automating a workflow — I follow the same instinctive loop. It's the same loop that makes a great office and people experience function work.

01
Observe

Something feels off, or an opportunity looks obvious when others miss it. I notice.

02
Question

I leave no room for assumptions. I define what I know vs. what I'm inferring. Actual critical thinking.

03
Research

Deep dives across disciplines — I use AI tools, primary sources, and domain experts.

04
Test

Empirically. I try things, document results, and talk to people for real evidence.

05
Synthesize

Patterns emerge. I distill complexity into something clear, actionable, human.

06
Share

I communicate findings differently for different audiences. Always with empathy.
